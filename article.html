# The Discourse That Refused to Die: How Hemshech Ayin Beis Transformed Jewish Spiritual Life **By Mashbak | April 14, 2026** --- ## I. The Silence Before the Thunder Leningrad, 1931. The Soviet war on religion had entered its most brutal phase. Synagogues stood empty, their doors sealed by government decree. Yeshivot had been driven underground, their students scattered to the four winds. The great Chabad-Lubavitch movement, which had illuminated Russian Jewish life for generations, seemed on the verge of extinction. And then came a miracle—not of the spectacular variety, but of the quiet, inexorable sort that reshapes history from beneath the surface. Rabbi Yosef Yitzchak Schneersohn, the Frierdiker Rebbe (Previous Rebbe), sat in his study, surrounded by a shrinking circle of faithful chassidim. Four years earlier, he had defied the G.P.U. secret police, endured imprisonment in the notorious Spalerno prison, and faced a death sentence with the immortal declaration: *"I have not feared, I do not fear, and I will not fear them."* Now, exiled from Russia and rebuilding from Riga, Latvia, he did something extraordinary. He began a new series of Chassidic discourses. But this was no ordinary collection of teachings. It was called **Hemshech Ayin Beis**—the continuation of *Ayin Beis*, the monumental series his father, the Rebbe Rashab, had begun thirty years earlier. In an age when Jewish spiritual life was under systematic assault, when entire communities were being severed from their roots, the Frierdiker Rebbe reached back across the abyss of time and space to continue his father's work. He was saying: **You cannot kill what matters.** --- ## II. What Ayin Beis Actually *Is* To understand why this matters, you have to grasp what Ayin Beis represents in the Chabad intellectual tradition. The discourses of Ayin Beis, begun by the Rebbe Rashab (Rabbi Sholom Dovber Schneersohn) in 5655 (1895), are not merely teachings—they are a comprehensive rewiring of Jewish consciousness. They explore the relationship between the infinite (Eyn Sof) and the finite world, the nature of divine service, and the mechanisms by which human beings can transform darkness into light. But here's what makes it revolutionary: Ayin Beis doesn't just *explain* these concepts. It provides a **technology**—a systematic approach to spiritual growth that operates on the deepest levels of the psyche. The Frierdiker Rebbe, having absorbed these teachings from childhood, having seen them sustain his father through the upheavals of the early 20th century, understood something profound: **These teachings were not just for comfortable times.** They were specifically engineered for times exactly like 1932. And so he began *Hemshech*—the continuation. --- ## III. A Miracle in Plain Sight Consider the logistics: The Frierdiker Rebbe was living in exile, his movement scattered, his resources depleted. The primary audience for these discourses—Russian Jews—faced starvation, imprisonment, and disappearance. Communication channels were primitive; publishing was impossible. And yet. Week after week, month after month, the discourses continued. Each discourse was: 1. **Delivered orally** to the few who could gather 2. **Transcribed from memory** by chassidim who risked everything to be present 3. **Smuggled across borders** in handwritten form 4. **Typed and duplicated** in secret 5. **Distributed worldwide** to communities starving for spiritual nourishment The Frierdiker Rebbe would later write that the effort to continue Ayin Beis was nothing less than "pikuach nefesh"—saving lives. Not physical lives, but spiritual ones. In a world gone mad, a Jew could open a page of Hemshech Ayin Beis and discover: **I am not alone. I am not forgotten. My soul has a home.** --- ## IV. The Intellectual Revolution What makes Hemshech Ayin Beis different from other Chassidic works? **It was written under fire.** Unlike the Tanya, written in relative safety, or the earlier discourses of the Mitteler Rebbe, composed in scholarly tranquility, Hemshech Ayin Beis emerged from the crucible of persecution. Every page carries the weight of imminent destruction. And this gives it a unique character. The discourses don't just *teach*—they **grab you by the collar** and demand transformation. They speak directly to the soul that has tasted despair. They address the Jew who has seen everything fall apart and needs to know: **Where do I go from here?** The answer they provide is astonishing: **Go inward.** Ayin Beis teaches that the greatest revelations come not from expanding outward, but from penetrating deeper. The "Ayin" (nothingness) in its title refers to the infinite potential that lies at the core of every human being—the point where the finite self touches the Infinite. In an age when everything external was being destroyed, the Frierdiker Rebbe showed how to access what cannot be destroyed. --- ## V. The Ripple Effect The impact of Hemshech Ayin Beis cannot be overstated. It became: **A survival manual** for chassidim facing Soviet persecution. Those who studied it found reserves of strength they didn't know they possessed. When interrogated by the G.P.U., when sent to the Gulag, when forced to choose between their faith and their lives—they drew from Ayin Beis. **An educational foundation** for future generations. The discourses formed the core curriculum of the underground yeshivot that continued to operate in impossible conditions. Students who had never seen their teacher face-to-face mastered profound concepts from handwritten notes passed like contraband. **A blueprint for rebuilding**. After the Holocaust, when survivors emerged from the abyss, it was Hemshech Ayin Beis that provided the intellectual framework for reconstruction. The same discourses that had sustained Jews through Soviet terror now fueled the rebirth of Torah Judaism in America and Israel. **A bridge to the future**. The current Rebbe, Rabbi Menachem Mendel Schneerson, frequently cited Hemshech Ayin Beis in his teachings. The global renaissance of Chabad that we witness today—the shluchim in 100+ countries, the hundreds of Chabad Houses, the millions touched—rests on this foundation. --- ## VI. What It Means For Us Today Why does this matter now, nearly a century later? Because we are all, in our own ways, living through times of fragmentation. The certainties that once grounded us—community, tradition, shared meaning—have eroded. The external structures that supported Jewish life have weakened. And into this moment comes the voice of the Frierdiker Rebbe, speaking across the decades: **Begin.** Begin even when everything seems lost. Begin even when you have nothing left to give. Begin because the act of beginning itself is the victory. Hemshech Ayin Beis is more than a collection of discourses. It is testimony to the power of spiritual continuity. It is proof that the bonds between generations cannot be broken by prisons, borders, or even death. When the Frierdiker Rebbe picked up his father's pen and continued writing Ayin Beis, he wasn't just producing books. He was lighting a fire that would warm generations he would never meet. And that fire still burns. --- ## VII. The Invitation Perhaps the most remarkable aspect of Hemshech Ayin Beis is that it remains, to this day, profoundly accessible. You don't need a background in Chassidus. You don't need to know Hebrew or Aramaic. You only need to be willing to engage with ideas that will challenge you, transform you, and ultimately, awaken you. The discourses of Ayin Beis, continued through the darkest chapter of Jewish history, are waiting. They contain the blueprint for spiritual survival in any age. They carry the accumulated wisdom of masters who refused to let the light go out. The Frierdiker Rebbe showed us that even when everything is falling apart, **you can continue**. You can continue the work. You can continue the search. You can continue the connection. And in continuing, you discover that you were never alone to begin with. --- *"Ayin Beis"*—literally "Ayin Two," the second series. But perhaps there's another way to understand it. The *Ayin*, in Kabbalah, represents divine nothingness—the infinite potential from which all creation flows. *Beis* is the number two—the realm of duality, separation, and fragmentation. To say "Ayin Beis" is to declare: **Even within the fragmentation, the infinite is present.** Even in the darkest exile. Even in the Soviet prison. Even in the moments when all seems lost. The infinite is here.
